1.1 Probability and Events

When working with probability we need three basic ingredients1

  1. The sample space \Omega. all possible outcomes of an experiment.
    Example: for a coin toss, \Omega = \{\text{Heads}, \text{Tails}\}.
  2. Events subsets of the sample space. We use capital letters A, B, \ldots to denote events.
    • The complement of A is A^c, meaning A does not occur.
    • The intersection A \cap B means A and B occur.
    • The union A \cup B means A or B (or both) occur.
  3. A probability distribution \mathrm{Pr}. Assigns numbers between 0 and 1 to events, with \mathrm{Pr}(\Omega)=1.

We can write the definitions of complement, intersection and union formally with sets:

Definition 1.1 (Complement, Intersection and Union)  

  • Complement: A^c = \{x \in \Omega \mid x \notin A \}.
  • Intersection: A\cap B = \{x \in \Omega \mid x \in A \text{ and } x \in B\}.
  • Union: A\cup B = \{x \in \Omega \mid x \in A \text{ or } x \in B\}.

Definition 1.2 (Addition rule (inclusion–exclusion)) For any two events A, B\subseteq \Omega, we have \mathrm{Pr}(A \cup B) = \mathrm{Pr}(A) + \mathrm{Pr}(B) - \mathrm{Pr}(A \cap B).

Example 1.1 (A quick inclusion–exclusion check) Roll a fair six-sided die. Let A=\{\text{even}\} and B=\{\text{number} \ge 4\}. Compute \mathrm{Pr}(A\cup B).

Solution

We have

  • \mathrm{Pr}(A)=3/6=1/2 (even outcomes 2,4,6).
  • \mathrm{Pr}(B)=3/6=1/2 (outcomes 4,5,6).
  • \mathrm{Pr}(A\cap B)=2/6=1/3 (outcomes 4,6).

By inclusion–exclusion 1.2, \mathrm{Pr}(A\cup B)=\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{2}-\tfrac{1}{3}=\tfrac{2}{3}.

Definition 1.3 (Mutually Exclusive, Exhaustive Events, and Partitions)  

  • Two events A and B are mutually exclusive (or disjoint) if they cannot occur together: \mathrm{Pr}(A \cap B) = 0.

  • Two events A and B are exhaustive if at least one of them must occur: \mathrm{Pr}(A \cup B) = 1.
    More generally, a collection of events \{B_1,\ldots,B_k\} is exhaustive if \bigcup_{i=1}^k B_i = \Omega, where \Omega is the sample space.

  • A collection of events \{B_1,\ldots,B_k\} forms a partition of \Omega if they are both mutually exclusive and exhaustive. Equivalently,

    • B_i \cap B_j = \varnothing for all i \neq j (non-overlapping), and
    • \bigcup_{i=1}^k B_i = \Omega (their union is the whole sample space).

Definition 1.4 (Conditional probability) If \mathrm{Pr}(B) > 0, the probability of A given B is \mathrm{Pr}(A \mid B) = \frac{\mathrm{Pr}(A \cap B)}{\mathrm{Pr}(B)}.

Definition 1.5 (Independence) Two events A and B are independent if knowing that one occurs does not change the probability of the other: \mathrm{Pr}(A \cap B) = \mathrm{Pr}(A)\mathrm{Pr}(B). Equivalently, \mathrm{Pr}(A\mid B) = \mathrm{Pr}(A) \quad \text{and} \quad \mathrm{Pr}(B\mid A) = \mathrm{Pr}(B)

Theorem 1.1 (Law of Total Probability) If B_1,\dots,B_k form a partition 1.3 of the sample space (mutually exclusive and exhaustive events), then for any event A: \mathrm{Pr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^k \mathrm{Pr}(A \mid B_i)\mathrm{Pr}(B_i).

NoteProof

Proof 1.1. Since B_1\ldots, B_n form a partition of \Omega, we can decompose any event A in a disjoint union of intersections: A = \bigcup_{i=1}^n (A\cap B_i). By induction on the addition rule 1.2, we thus have \mathrm{Pr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathrm{Pr}(A\cap B_i). By the definition of conditional probability 1.4, we have \mathrm{Pr}(A\cap B_i) = \mathrm{Pr}(A\mid B_i) \mathrm{Pr}(B_i). Therefore,

\mathrm{Pr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathrm{Pr}(A \mid B_i)\mathrm{Pr}(B_i).

Theorem 1.2 (Bayes’ Theorem) If \mathrm{Pr}(B) > 0, then \mathrm{Pr}(A \mid B) = \frac{\mathrm{Pr}(B \mid A)\mathrm{Pr}(A)}{\mathrm{Pr}(B)}.

NoteProof

Proof 1.2. From the definition of conditional probability 1.4,
\mathrm{Pr}(A \mid B) = \frac{\mathrm{Pr}(A \cap B)}{\mathrm{Pr}(B)}.

But the intersection A \cap B can also be written the other way round: \mathrm{Pr}(A \cap B) = \mathrm{Pr}(B \cap A).

Using the definition of conditional probability again, \mathrm{Pr}(B \cap A) = \mathrm{Pr}(B \mid A)\mathrm{Pr}(A).

Substituting this into the first expression gives \mathrm{Pr}(A \mid B) = \frac{\mathrm{Pr}(B \mid A)\mathrm{Pr}(A)}{\mathrm{Pr}(B)}.

This completes the proof.

Example 1.2 (Student Example) Seventy percent of students are attentive in lectures and thirty percent are not. If a student is attentive the probability of passing the course is 0.8. If a student is not attentive the probability of passing the course is 0.1.

  1. A student is selected at random. What is the probability that they pass the course?
  2. Given that the student passed the course, what is the probability that they were attentive?
Solution

Let

  • P denote the event of a student passing the course.
  • A denote the event of a student being attentive.
  • A^c denote the event of a student not being attentive.
  1. Attentive and inattentive form a partition. By the law of total probability,

\mathrm{Pr}(P)=\mathrm{Pr}(P\mid A)\mathrm{Pr}(A)+\mathrm{Pr}(P\mid A^c)\mathrm{Pr}(A^c)=0.8\times0.7+0.1\times0.3=0.59.

  1. Using Bayes’ theorem,

\mathrm{Pr}(A\mid P)=\frac{\mathrm{Pr}(P\mid A)\mathrm{Pr}(A)}{\mathrm{Pr}(P)} =\frac{0.8\times0.7}{0.59}=0.949\ \text{(3 s.f.)}.

1.2 Random Variables

We use upper-case letters for random variables, e.g. X, Y, X_1, X_2,\dots.

  • Cumulative distribution function (cdf). For any (real-valued) random variable X,

    F(x)=\mathrm{Pr}(X\le x).

  • Discrete case (pmf). If X is discrete, its probability mass function is

    p(x)=\mathrm{Pr}(X=x),\qquad \sum_x p(x)=1.

  • Continuous case (pdf). If X is continuous, its probability density function satisfies

    f(x)=\frac{\partial F(x)}{\partial x},\qquad \mathrm{Pr}(a<X\le b)=\int_a^b f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x,\qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=1.

Example 1.3 (Discrete CDF.) Let X be the outcome of a fair six-sided die. Find p(x) and F(3).

Solution

p(x)=\mathrm{Pr}(X=x)=1/6 for x\in\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}, and 0 otherwise.
F(3)=\mathrm{Pr}(X\le 3)=\mathrm{Pr}(\{1,2,3\})=3\times(1/6)=1/2.

Example 1.4 Let X\sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0,1). Compute \mathrm{Pr}(0.2<X<0.5).

Solution

Here f(x)=1 for 0<x<1 and 0 otherwise.

\mathrm{Pr}(0.2<X<0.5)=\int_{0.2}^{0.5} 1\,dx = 0.3.

1.2.1 Joint, Marginal, and Conditional Densities

Suppose X and Y are random variables with joint PDF/PMF f_{XY}(x,y) (often written f(x,y)). From this joint distribution we can define:

  • Marginal distributions: the distribution of each variable on its own.
  • Conditional distributions: the distribution of one variable given the other.
  • Independence: the case where the joint factorises.

Definition 1.6 (Marginal Distributions) If we only care about X, regardless of Y, we integrate/sum out Y: f_X(x) = \int f(x,y)\,\mathrm{d}y. Similarly for f_Y(y).
Note: knowing the marginals does not determine the joint.

Definition 1.7 (Conditional Densities) For y in the support of Y with f_Y(y) > 0, we define: f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y) = \frac{f(x,y)}{f_Y(y)}. This describes the distribution of X given Y=y.

Theorem 1.3 (Law of Total Probability) We can recover the marginal by “averaging” the conditional over Y: f_X(x) = \int f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y) f_Y(y)\,\mathrm{d}y.

Definition 1.8 (Independence) X and Y are independent if and only if f(x,y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y). In this case, f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y) = f_X(x): knowing Y tells us nothing about X.

1.3 Expectations, Variances and Covariances

Definition 1.9 (Expected Value) The expected value (or population mean) of X is \mathrm{E}[X] = \begin{cases} \displaystyle \sum_{\text{all } x} x\,p_X(x), & \text{if $X$ is discrete}, \\ \displaystyle \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\,f_X(x)\,\mathrm{d}x, & \text{if $X$ is continuous}. \end{cases}

We often write \mu = \mathrm{E}[X]. This is a measure of location.

Definition 1.10 (Expectation of g(X)) Let g(\cdot) be any function and X a random variable with PMF p_X (discrete) or PDF f_X (continuous). The expected value of g(X) is \mathrm{E}[g(X)] = \begin{cases} \displaystyle \sum_{\text{all } x} g(x)\,p_X(x), & \text{if $X$ is discrete},\\ \displaystyle \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x)\,f_X(x)\,\mathrm{d}x, & \text{if $X$ is continuous}. \end{cases}

Definition 1.11 (Expectation (Bivariate)) If (X,Y) is a pair of random variables with joint PMF p_{X,Y} (discrete) or joint PDF f_{X,Y} (continuous), then for any function g(x,y) \mathrm{E}[g(X,Y)] = \begin{cases} \displaystyle \sum_{\text{all } x}\sum_{\text{all } y} g(x,y)\,p_{X,Y}(x,y), & \text{if $(X,Y)$ are discrete},\\ \displaystyle \int\!\!\!\int g(x,y)\,f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,\mathrm{d}x\,\mathrm{d}y, & \text{if $(X,Y)$ are continuous}. \end{cases}

Definition 1.12 (Moments) The quantities \mathrm{E}[X], \mathrm{E}[X^2], \mathrm{E}[X^3], \ldots are the (raw) moments of X.

Definition 1.13 (Variance) The population variance of X is \mathrm{Var}(X) = \mathrm{E}\!\left[(X-\mathrm{E}[X])^2\right] = \mathrm{E}[X^2] - \big(\mathrm{E}[X]\big)^2. We often write \sigma^2 = \mathrm{Var}(X). This is a measure of spread.

Definition 1.14 (Standard Deviation) The population standard deviation is the positive square root of the variance: \mathrm{SD}(X) = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X)}. We often write \sigma = \mathrm{SD}(X). It shares the same units as X and measures spread.

Definition 1.15 (Covariance) For two random variables X and Y, the covariance is \mathrm{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathrm{E}\!\left[(X-\mathrm{E}[X])(Y-\mathrm{E}[Y])\right] = \mathrm{E}[XY] - \mathrm{E}[X]\mathrm{E}[Y]. It measures linear association: positive if Y tends to increase with X; negative if Y tends to decrease as X increases.

Definition 1.16 (Correlation) The correlation (often denoted \rho) is the scaled covariance \rho = \mathrm{Corr}(X,Y) = \frac{\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)}{\mathrm{SD}(X)\,\mathrm{SD}(Y)}. Correlation always satisfies \rho \in [-1,1].

Theorem 1.4 (Independence \Rightarrow Zero Covariance) If X and Y are independent, then \mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)=0.

Note. The converse need not hold: it is possible to have \mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)=0 while X and Y are not independent.

Example 1.5 (Variance of a Bernoulli Random Variable) A Bernoulli random variable 1.1 X takes only the values 0 and 1, with probabilities \operatorname{Pr}(X=1) = p, \qquad \operatorname{Pr}(X=0) = 1-p, \qquad 0 \leq p \leq 1.

For what value of p is the variance maximised?

Solution

We compute the mean and variance of X.

\mathrm{E}[X] = 1 \cdot p + 0 \cdot (1-p) = p,

\mathrm{E}[X^2] = 1^2 \cdot p + 0^2 \cdot (1-p) = p.

Therefore, \mathrm{Var}(X) = \mathrm{E}[X^2] - \big(\mathrm{E}[X]\big)^2 = p - p^2 = p(1-p).

To find the value of p that maximises this variance, consider the function f(p) = p(1-p), \quad 0 \leq p \leq 1.

Differentiate: f'(p) = 1 - 2p.

Setting f'(p) = 0 gives p = \tfrac{1}{2}.

Second derivative: f''(p) = -2 < 0, so this is indeed a maximum.

Finally, check the boundary values: f(0) = 0, \qquad f(1) = 0, \qquad f\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \tfrac{1}{4}.

Conclusion.
The variance of a Bernoulli random variable is maximised at
p = \tfrac{1}{2}, \quad \mathrm{Var}(X) = \tfrac{1}{4}.

1.4.1 Convergence of Random Variables

Definition 1.17 (Convergence in Probability) A sequence of random variables X_n is said to converge in probability to a random variable X if, for every \varepsilon > 0,

\Pr\big(|X_n - X| > \varepsilon\big) \;\longrightarrow\; 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty.

We write this as X_n \xrightarrow{p} X.

Intuitively: as n grows, the probability that X_n is far from X becomes negligible.

Definition 1.18 (Convergence in Distribution) A sequence of random variables X_n is said to converge in distribution to a random variable X if, for all continuity points x of F_X,

F_{X_n}(x) \;\longrightarrow\; F_X(x) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty,

where F_{X_n} and F_X are the cumulative distribution functions.

We write this as X_n \xrightarrow{d} X.

Intuitively: the distribution of X_n “settles down” and approaches that of X.

NoteRelationship

Convergence in probability is stronger than convergence in distribution:
X_n \xrightarrow{p} X \;\;\;\implies\;\;\; X_n \xrightarrow{d} X. But not the other way around.

1.4.2 Probability Inequalities

Proposition 1.1 (Chebyshev’s Inequality) If a random variable X has mean \mu and variance \sigma^2 < \infty, then for any \varepsilon > 0,

\Pr\big(|X - \mu| \geq \varepsilon\big) \;\leq\; \frac{\sigma^2}{\varepsilon^2}.

This provides a bound on the probability of large deviations from the mean.

1.4.3 Factorising the Joint

Proposition 1.2 (The Chain Rule) Let \underline{X} = (X_1,\ldots, X_n) with joint PDF/PMF f(\underline{x}). Then f(\underline{x}) \;=\; f(x_1)\;\prod_{t=2}^n f \big(x_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1}\big).

NoteProof (Not Examinable)

Proof 1.3 (Not Examinable). We use the definition of conditional PDF/PMF (Definition 1.7) and induction.

  • Base case (n=2). By definition, f(x_2\mid x_1) \;=\; \frac{f(x_1,x_2)}{f(x_1)} \quad \text{(for $f(x_1)>0$),} hence f(x_1,x_2)=f(x_1)\,f(x_2\mid x_1).

  • Inductive step. For k\ge 2, f(x_1,\ldots,x_k) \;=\; f(x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1})\, f(x_k\mid x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1}), again by Definition 1.7: f(x_k\mid x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1}) \;=\; \frac{f(x_1,\ldots,x_k)}{f(x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1})}. Iterating this factorisation from k=2 up to k=n yields f(x_1,\ldots,x_n) \;=\; f(x_1)\prod_{t=2}^n f(x_t\mid x_1,\ldots,x_{t-1}).

Telescoping viewpoint. Equivalently, \prod_{t=2}^n f(x_t\mid x_1,\ldots,x_{t-1}) = \prod_{t=2}^n \frac{f(x_1,\ldots,x_t)}{f(x_1,\ldots,x_{t-1})} = \frac{f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)}{f(x_1)}, so rearranging gives the same result.

Remarks.

  • The argument applies to both PMFs and PDFs (assuming the relevant conditionals exist).
  • The identity does not assume independence; it is always valid wherever the conditionals are defined.
